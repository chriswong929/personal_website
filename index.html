travlr

MHacks - January 2015 - University of Michigan

Have you ever wanted to travel, but experience a new city in a complete unique way? What if you could have a local show you around all the ins and outs of the city? travlr is a web application that connects people travelling to new cities to local in the area, who act as tour guides and show people around.

Our team at MHacks decided to design a web application that provides a new way of connecting tourists with people living in the area. After entering a destination location and a specified date, a list of locals who are free to show people around will be revealed to the user. The user can then filter this list by age, languages spoken, and personal interests to find the best local that best matches the user's personality. Clicking on a local's profile will display their basic information, a short bio, and a map of their favorite places in the city. travlr provides the platform for any user to act as a "local" and show people around the city they love. This app could also potentially be used to connect tourists and real tour guides and tour guide companies in the area as well.

The front end of this app was developed using Jade and Stylus while the backend was developed using NodeJS, Express, and MongoDB. This app is still a working progress. Please stay tuned for its official release!

SoilAlert

YHacks - October 2014 - Yale University

Clean, safe drinking water is scarce. In our world today, nearly 1 billion people in developing countries do not have access to clean water. Water scarcity is becoming a prevalent problem in today's world and is even noticeable in many parts of the United States.

Our team at YHacks decided to address this issue by designing a system that reduces water consumption in everyday irrigation systems. We designed SoilAlert, a hardware-based dynamic irrigation system developed using the Intel Galileo Gen 2 microprocessor. The Intel Microprocessor was connected to a series of sensors that collected real-time data about water levels in the soil. These sensors were evenly spaced throughout a large piece of land and each sensor was responsible for a specific area of the land and provided important information about its water levels. Whenever one of these sensors detected water levels below a threshold value, the Intel Microprocessor first determined the piece of land that the sensor is responsible for and then sent a signal to turn on the sprinklers for that piece of land. This process could significantly reduce the water consumption levels found in standard irrigation system because it only waters parts of the land that need watering, rather than turning on the water system for the entire property.

The sensors placed in various parts throughout the land represented a distributed system that was capable of collecting and forwarding real-time data to a microprocessor. Because our system only waters parts of the land that are drier than a given threshold level, rather than watering the entire property, we reduce water consumption in common irrigation systems, which benefits not only the environment but also the users who can save money by cutting down on their water use.

SoilAlert was built on NodeJS and the data collected by the sensors was stored using Firebase. In addition to the hardware, we created an accompanying web application that utilized D3's data visualization API, which allowed us to display the information collected by the sensors in an intuitive manner to the end user.

HackTheNorth - September 2014 - University of Waterloo

PlateShare is a web application built on NodeJS and MongoDB that my team created for HackTheNorth. Our team realized that many restaurants provide a food delivery service that requires a minimum order amount of $20+. Users who only want to spend $10 can use PlateShare to connect with other hungry users in the neighbourhood and place orders together in order to reach the minimum order amount. PlateShare helps our users cut down food delivery costs by combining different orders to the same restaurant placed in the same neighbourhood.

Once a user logs in, PlateShare searches for all food orders placed in the user's vicinity and displays them on a map. Our user then has the option to add to any order seen on the map, essentially combining two orders to the same restaurant into one.

Not only does PlateShare help users cut down delivery costs, but the app also benefits restaurant owners. Restaurants can use PlateShare to attract new customers and increase revenue. PlateShare also increases the efficiency of the delivery process because once two orders are combined, a driver will only need to deliver to one address instead of two.

Chrome Reminders

C3! Hackathon - February 2014 - University of Toronto

Chrome Reminders was my first experience with HTML, CSS, and JavaScript and was built at my first ever hackathon. This Chrome Extension allows users to set reminders within Chrome by implementing URL links that bring users back to a specific webpage. With Chrome Reminders, users can organize and manage internet tasks from right within the browser (no need to open external applications). In addition, your reminders are synced with your Google account and you can access them on any computer that has the extension installed. This app was selected as a finalist to present in front of judges at the end of the hackathon.

Digital System Design

November 2013

As part of a school project, I helped design a large digital system in Verilog using programmable logic on an FPGA chip. The system utilized Altera's DE2 Board and accepted PS2 keyboard inputs and the output was displayed on the VGA. The project was a game inspired by Angry Birds and the core gameplay involved launching the iconic red bird and hitting moving targets. The digital system was based on state machine transitions and I personally wrote code to build the physics engine and the control system that governed how different components of the system interacted.
